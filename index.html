Aliasing-Free Neural Audio Synthesis

Yicheng Gu1,2,3 Junan Zhang3 Chaoren Wang3 Jerry Li1 Zhizheng Wu3 Lauri Juvela2
1 Spellbrush 2 Aalto University 3 The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen

Abstract

Neural vocoder and codec models reconstruct time-domain waveforms from acoustic representations, which directly affect the sound quality in audio generation systems. Among various approaches, upsampling-based time-domain models are superior in both inference speed and synthesis quality, achieving the state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. Nevertheless, despite their success in producing perceptually natural sound, their synthesis fidelity remains limited due to the aliasing artifacts brought by the inadequately designed model architectures. In particular, the unconstrained non-linear activation functions will generate infinite amounts of harmonics that exceed the Nyquist frequency, resulting in ``folded-back'' aliasing artifacts. Meanwhile, the widely used upsampling layer, ConvTranspose, will copy and stretch the mirrored low-frequency parts to fill the empty high-frequency region, leading to ``mirrored'' aliasing artifacts. Moreover, its inherent periodicity will also introduce ``tonal artifacts'', resulting in constant frequency ringings. This paper aims to resolve these issues from a signal processing perspective. Specifically, we apply anti-derivative anti-aliasing (ADAA) to the activation function to obtain its anti-aliased form, and replace the problematic ConvTranspose layer with resampling to avoid the tonal artifacts and eliminate the aliased components. Based on our proposed anti-aliased modules, we introduce Pupu-Vocoder and Pupu-Codec to facilitate audio generation research. We constructed a test signal benchmark to illustrate the effectiveness of the anti-aliased modules and conducted experiments on speech, singing voice, music, and audio to show the effectiveness of our proposed models. Experimental results confirm that our light-weighted Pupu-Vocoder and Pupu-Codec models can easily outperform the large-scale BigVGAN and BigCodec models with the proposed theoretical development.

Tutorial

What is Aliasing?

In signal processing and related disciplines, aliasing is a phenomenon in which a reconstructed signal from samples of the original signal contains low-frequency components that are not present in the original signal. Aliasing occurs due to the constraint of discrete sampling. According to the Fourier Theorem, any periodic signal can be represented as a sum of sinusoidal components with different frequencies, amplitudes, and phases. Suppose we have a fixed sampling frequency. When the frequency of the sinusoidal component is minor, we can accurately reconstruct the original signal from the discretely sampled points. When the frequency of the sinusoidal component exceeds a specific value, known as the Nyquist frequency, which is half of the sampling frequency, we cannot reconstruct the original signal from the discretely sampled points; instead, we reconstruct it as a sinusoidal component with a lower frequency that is not present in the original signal, a phenomenon known as aliasing, as illustrated in the figure above.

Why does Aliasing Occur?

The existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) vocoder and codec systems are upsampling-based time-domain models. In particular, they take in a spectrogram or a spectrogram-like latent representation, convert it into a multi-channel waveform template, and then repeat the process of upsampling and channel fusion, applying ResBlocks and activations to the template until it reaches the exact resolution as the waveform, as illustrated in the figure above.

However, from the view of digital signal processing, such a paradigm has numerous inherent problems that hinder the upper limit regarding model performance. This is a limitation in the architectural design itself, which cannot be overcome regardless of how large we scale the dataset size or model capacity.

Let us discuss some of the significant artifacts brought by these inadequately designed model architectures below:

"Folded-Back" Aliasing

Firstly, the use of unconstrained nonlinear activation functions introduces an infinite number of harmonic components, where the harmonic components with frequencies higher than the Nyquist frequency become "folded-back" aliasing artifacts. Following Wavehax, we take the ReLU activation as an example. Suppose the input signal is a sine wave with angular frequency \(\omega\) for continuous time \(t \in R\), after applying ReLU, the resulting signal's Fourier expansion becomes:

$$ \text{relu}(\sin(\omega t)) = \frac{1}{\pi} + \frac{\sin(\omega t)}{2} - \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{2\cos(2k \omega t)}{\pi(2k - 1)(2k + 1)}, $$
where the last term induces an infinite amount of harmonics. The frequency components higher than the Nyquist frequency, where\(\frac{k \omega }{\pi} > \frac{F_{N}}{2}\), would become the aliasing artifacts, which is illustrated as the orange pitch contour in the figure above.

"Mirrored" Aliasing

The widely used upsampling layer, ConvTranspose, is implemented equivalent to inserting zeros into the input signal and then applying channel fusion dilated convolutions. In the time-frequency domain, such a process is comparable to copying and stretching the mirrored low-frequency components to fill the empty high-frequency region. Since the mirrored frequency components do not exist in the original signal, they become harsh noises that significantly degrade the signal. This phenomenon is known as the "mirrored" aliasing artifacts, which is the green pitch contour in the figure above.

"Tonal Artifact"

The inherent computational periodicity of the ConvTranspose layer will also lead to "tonal artifacts" when the layer parameters are trainable and poorly initialized, resulting in constant frequency ringings that significantly degrade the signal fidelity, as illustrated by the purple constant frequency ringings in the figure above. Better parameter initialization strategies cannot easily solve this phenomenon, nor does it disappear after the model converges. For a more detailed analysis of the exact cause of this problem, please refer to this paper and this paper.

"Filter Artifact"

To address the "tonal" artifacts, previous work proposes using linear and nearest interpolation layers as alternatives, since they do not have trainable parameters and will not introduce "tonal" artifacts. Meanwhile, since their computational processes are equivalent to low-pass filtering, they can also remove part of the "mirrored" aliasing artifacts. However, if we examine their frequency responses individually, as shown in the figure above (Left: Linear Interpolation; Right: Nearest Interpolation), we can observe that their corresponding frequency responses do not actually filter out all the "mirrored" aliasing artifacts. Moreover, according to their filter shapes, the existing low-frequency components we want to retain are also filtered, as shown in the red rectangles. If we pass two white noises through two consecutive linear and nearest interpolation layers with an upsampling factor of two, the result will have empty frequency bands, as illustrated in the figure above (Left: Linear Interpolation; Right: Nearest Interpolation), which is known as "filter artifacts."

Methodology

To achieve a higher synthesis fidelity, this work introduces anti-aliased and artifact-free activation and upsampling modules, as we discussed following:
Anti-Aliased Activation Function

We use the oversampling technique and apply anti-derivative anti-aliasing (ADAA) to the activation function to obtain its anti-aliased form.

Oversampling

Oversampling is a technique that temporally increases the Nyquist frequency before applying the non-linear activation function, and then downsamples the signal back after filtering out the unwanted frequency components using a low-pass filter, as illustrated in the figure above. To achieve a good trade-off between quality and efficiency, our proposed models use an oversampling factor of two.

Anti-Derivative Anti-Aliasing (ADAA)

Anti-derivative anti-aliasing (ADAA) overcomes the sampling frequency constraint, thereby avoiding aliasing. To implement ADAA, we first interpolate the input discrete signal into a continuous one and then apply the non-linear activation function. We then remove the unwanted frequency bands using a low-pass filter and discretely sample the signal back to obtain the output. Under such a scenario, the activation function will not introduce aliasing artifacts since it is applied to a continuous signal. When we use linear interpolation and a rectangular kernel for low-pass filtering, we can obtain a closed form as follows:

$$ y_t = \frac{F(x_t) - F(x_{t - 1})}{x_t - x_{t - 1}}, $$
where \(x\) is the input signal, \(t\) is the discrete index, \(F( \cdot )\) is the first order anti-derivative of the activation function, and \(y\) is the output signal. Following BigVGAN and DAC, We use SnakeBeta as the activation function to utilize is periodic nature, which is:
$$ f(x) = x + \frac{\sin(\alpha x)^2}{\beta}, $$
which, applying ADAA gives the following closed form:
$$ y_t = \frac{1}{2\beta} + \frac{x_t + x_{t - 1}}{2} - \frac{\cos(\alpha(x_t + x_{t - 1}))\text{sinc}(\alpha(x_t - x_{t - 1}))}{2\beta}, $$
in which the detailed derivative process and analysis on its gradient stability can be found in our paper.

Anti-Aliased Upsampling Layer

As shown in the figure above, we replace the ConvTranspose layer with resampling (zero insertion + low-pass filter) to avoid the "tonal" artifacts and eliminate the aliased components. We apply a channel expansion Conv1D layer with a high-pass filter to convert the zero-inserted \(x_0\) to a noise-like deterministic prior, which is used to fill the empty high-frequency region of the upsampled signal to improve the training stability (\(x_0\) is the latent representation obtained from the first Conv1D layer in the decoder). The added-up full-band signal is then fed to a channel expansion Conv1D layer to get the layer output.

Pupu-Vocoder and Pupu-Codec

Based on our proposed anti-aliased activation and upsampling modules, we further introduce the Pupu-Vocoder and Pupu-Codec models to facilitate audio generation research. The Pupu-Codec model is modified from the DAC, in which we replaced the activation and upsampling modules in the decoder with our proposed ones, and utilized two additional discriminators for better synthesis quality (Multi-Scale Discriminator (MSD) and Multi-Scale Sub-Band Constant-Q Transform Discriminator (MS-SB-CQTD)). Removing the encoder and residual vector quantizer (RVQ) modules from Pupu-Codec gives the Pupu-Vocoder model.
